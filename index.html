The Science, Made Simple

Your Spine Has a
Pain Switch.
Yours Is Stuck Open.

Your brain does not automatically feel everything that happens in your body. It has a gate, a filter in your spinal cord, that decides which pain signals reach your brain. When your spine is not moving correctly, that gate gets stuck open and pain flows through unchecked.

Your Pain Gate: Interactive
1
When your spine moves correctly, the gate stays shut
Healthy spinal joints fire mechanoreceptors constantly. Those sensors flood the gate with "all clear" signals, keeping it closed. Pain that does not deserve your attention never reaches your brain.
2
When your spine gets stuck, the gate swings open
Dysfunctional joints go quiet. With no "all clear" signal, the gate defaults open. Every pain signal, including old injuries, inflammation, and normal wear, flows straight through to your brain unchecked.
3
The adjustment fires the sensors and the gate closes
A precise chiropractic adjustment restores joint motion, fires the mechanoreceptors in the joint capsule, and the gate slams shut. This is Gate Control Theory, published in Science in 1965, confirmed by brain imaging research ever since.

But pain is only 10% of the story

Your Spine Controls
Far More Than Pain

Only about 10% of your nervous system is dedicated to pain signaling. The other 90% runs organ function, immunity, hormones, digestion, and motor control silently. When your spine is not functioning correctly, the effects extend far beyond what you feel.

Pain Gating
Mechanoreceptors in your spinal joints actively suppress pain signals at the spinal cord. When joints stop moving, that suppression fails and pain floods through. (Melzack and Wall, Science, 1965)
Brain Function
Spinal adjustment changes processing in the prefrontal cortex by nearly 20% on average. Your spine is your brain's primary source of proprioceptive data. (Lelic et al., Neural Plasticity, 2016)
Autonomic Regulation
Spinal dysfunction keeps your nervous system locked in sympathetic overdrive. Adjustment shifts it toward parasympathetic, restoring sleep, digestion, and immune function. (Picchiottino et al., 2021)
